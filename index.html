A Meta Zine Library

Zine libraries and distros in New York City

An academic definition: "A zine, short for fanzine or magazine, is a DIY & subculture self-publication, usually made on paper and reproduced with a photocopier or printer. Zine creators are often motivated by a desire to share knowledge or experience with people in marginalized or otherwise less-empowered communities"

A spunky definition: "Zines (pronounced "zeens," from fanzines) are cut-and-paste, "sorry this is late," self-published magazines reproduced at Kinko's or on the sly at work and distributed through mail order and word of mouth. They touch on sex, music, politics, television, movies, work, food, whatever. They're Tinkertoys for malcontents. They're obsessed with obsession. They're extraordinary and ordinary. They're about strangeness but since it's usually happening somewhere else you're kind of relieved. You can get to know people pretty well through their zines, which are always more personal and idiosyncratic than glossy magazines because glossies and the celebrities they worship are so busy being well known."

A little more about what zines are:

  • Zines are made for the love of creating and not for profit.
  • Independently published.
  • Can be physical (as simple as copy paper stapled together) or digital (like a website).
  • Can be made solo or with a group of people.
  • The contents can be about anything and everything you can imagine: personal stories ("perzines"), music-related writing, lists of things, political ideologies, photography, mental health and self-care tips, fiction, artwork, comics, and so on.

Zine Distros: Zines are acquired via zine distributors. Often, the distributor is the author themselves. However, zines are also distributed via independent booksellers, artist collectives, and community organizations. In the age of the internet, creators can submit zines to various organizations via email or form submission. These methods allow for zines to be shared to a greater audience.

Why are zines important?

  • Zines provide spaces for marginalized voices and stories that are excluded from traditional publishing.
  • Zines challenge us to think about authority in differing contexts.
  • Zines reframe topics we talk about in class and life.
  • Zines allow us to channel our thoughts creatively into art.

Check out Zine Book to learn more about zines and to access various resources.

Zines have been around since the early 20th century. The earliest zine in the U.S.A. dates back to 1930. It was a science fiction publication called The Coment, created by the Science Correspondence Club. The zine cultivated a community of science fiction fans as there was a letter section that provided space for discussion.

Science fiction fanzines continued to grow into the 1940s. A fanzine is a magazine produced by fans of a specific cultural phenomenon, the term being coined by Louis "Russ" Chauvenet, one of the founders of science fiction fandom. Science fiction authors such as Ray Bradbury, Jack Williamson, and Robert Heinlein created zines as well. In addition to the continued growth of science fiction zines, Beat Poetry chapbooks emerged in the 1940s, by poets such as Allen Ginsburg. The 1940s is also when the first ever queer fanzine was published by Edythe Eyde (aka Lisa Ben).

In the 1970s, punk fanzines and zines emerged. The 1990s saw the creation of the "Riot Grrrl" movement, which used zines as a medium to discuss feminism and issues surrounding gender identity.

Example 1: As I read about the early origins of zines, and its roots being science fiction, it made me think about our lecture on world building. For example, one of Ray Bradbury's most well known works is Fahrenheit 451, a story taking place in a dystopian future where books are outlawed and firemen start fires to burn any that are found. The protagonist of the story is a fireman who becomes disillusioned by the society he is living in, and his role within it, and attempts to preserve the books. The book was written during the McCarthy era and Second Red scare. It was inspired by the ideological repression exhibited in the Soviet Union, as well as book burnings in Nazi Germany. The early days of zines being embedded within the realm of science fiction showcases an interesting intersection between underground publishing, world building, and the development of an information undercommons.

Example 2: As mentioned above, the 1940s and 1950s saw an emergence of beat poetry chapbooks by poets such as Allen Ginsburg. A chapbook is a pocket-sized booklet. The origin of chapbooks dates back to the 17th century. Like zines, chapbooks were a critical medium for distributing popular culture to common folks. The Beat Generation is a literary subculture movement that explored and influenced American culture and politics post-World War II. The poets of this generation rebelled against mainstream American life and writing. The movement challenged the conventional structure of American society by questioning materialism, criticizing capitalism, and challenging taboos, particularly around sexuality. Beat poetry is a form of poetry that is surrealistic and mostly free verse. Underground music styles, such as jazz, were a source of inspiration being many of the beat poetry written during the time. The Beat Generation is an example of an undercommons - it was a social and cultural phenomenon that was led by a collective of writers and thinkers whom directly opposed mainstream culture and structure. The work that was created was distributed via underground publishing in the form of chapbooks, and was inspired by Jazz (an underground music style) and Native American spirituality (cultural influences from a marginalized group).(Source 1, Source 2)

Example 3: In 1950s Soviet Union, Samizdat was created. This was a literary publication that was written, copied, and distributed in secret. It was critical of Soviet ideology and government. These zines were manually reproduced (by hand!) because printing devices, such as the typewriter, required official registration and permission to access. Samizdat is a form of information undercommons because it is an underground literary publication that was created to challenging the Soviet Union, which had a strictly enforced censorship policy.

Example 4: The punk fanzines created during the 1970s to 1990s relied on zines to distribute information about music groups, venues, and events. Zines were used as a means of rejecting corporate labels and mainstream publications.

Example 5: Fire!! magazine was a literary magazine that was published during the Harlem Renaissance, an intellectual and cultural revival of African-American arts and culture centered in Harlem, NYC. Fire!!, among other "little magazines", were used by African-American artists and writers to undermine the established literary system. Fire!! discussed controversial issues such as sexuality and prostitution. It was one of the first magazines to be completely written and published by African Americans. It provided a platform for young Black authors (Source 1, Source 2).

Today, zines continue to serve as a means to distribute information, bring awareness, and initiate discussion about societal issues, controversial and taboo topics, and social/political/economic movements, among many other things. While social media has provided a way to disseminate information at a much quicker and larger scale, it doesn't provide true anonymity. ABC No Rio

Website

http://www.abcnorio.org/

About

"ABC No Rio is a collectively-run center for art and activism. We are known internationally as a venue for oppositional culture. ABC No Rio was founded in 1980 by artists committed to political and social engagement and we retain these values to the present. We seek to facilitate cross-pollination between artists and activists. ABC No Rio is a place where people share resources and ideas to impact society, culture, and community. We believe that art and activism should be for everyone, not just the professionals, experts, and cognoscenti. Our dream is a cadres of actively aware artists and artfully aware activists." (read more about ABC No Rio here)

Zine Library

http://www.abcnorio.org/facilities/zine_library.html

ABC No Rio's zine library primarily focuses on the following:

  • Zines that emerge from, or cover, NYC's Lower East Side and downtown Manhattan
  • Zines that are embedded in countercultures, subcultures, political scenes, DIY communities, and/or social movements
  • Zines that directly address social relations
  • Zines that engage in DIY practical advice giving, covering such areas as parenting, education, health, dating, and peer support

Some cool work conducted by ABC No Rio

  • InterActivist Network: Not active, however as of February 2021, is being revived. The InterActivist Network is a "...collaborative effort, an activist communication resource, an independent media project and a technology skill sharing project.Through the Info Exchange and Skills Share, we seek to offer new, dissenting and thoughtful perspectives on our own communities and to disseminate information about news-worthy events overlooked or misrepresented by mainstream media." The Skills Share houses some websites that were produced by the InterActivist Network, such as Collision of Interests: Traffic and Transportation, Bloody Legislation Against the Poor: The Struggle for Living Space in New York City, and Under The Asphalt: Community Gardens in New York City.
  • Food Not Bombs--New York City: "Food Not Bombs prepares and serves healthy vegan food to the homeless in Tompkins Square Park, and often serves at protests and demonstrations in the New York City area."
  • Take What You Can't Get: "Inspired by the role of artists as imaginators, and agitators, from the 1930s to the present day, Take What You Can't Get embraces our current moment of multilayered crisis (economic, racial justice, leadership, health) as an opportunity for artists to actively imagine a more desirable future. The exhibition's title addresses pre-existing artworld expectations of austerity and extreme inequality - that artists and art workers must take what they can get."

Booklyn

Website

https://booklyn.org/

About

Mission: "Booklyn’s mission is to promote artists’ books as art and research material and to assist artists and organizations in documenting, exhibiting, and distributing their artwork and archives within the academic market. We specifically assist artists and organizations committed to environmental and social justice. We work towards our mission by documenting, exhibiting, promoting, and distributing their work within educational institutions worldwide. We envision a world in which art and bookmaking are tools for education, personal agency, community engagement, and activism."

History: "Founded in 1999, Booklyn is an artist-run, non-profit 501(c)(3) organization headquartered at the Brooklyn Army Terminal in Sunset Park, Brooklyn. Over two decades, Booklyn has created a global network connecting hundreds of artists and organizations with educational institutions. Through that network, Booklyn has distributed thousands of books, artworks, archival collections, and box sets used by an audience reaching hundreds of thousands."

Read more about Brooklyn here.

Zine Distro

https://booklyn.org/services/

Booklyn provides a range of services to educational and cultural institutions, as well as to individual collectors and artists. among the services provided to artists are:

  • Fiscal Sponsorships
  • Bookbinding and Repair
  • General Consultation and Advising
  • Archival Case Design and Production Services
  • Documentation Services

Additionally, they have a form where one could submit their zine for consideration to be distributed within research and educational institutes, as well as their zine club, webstore to the general public, and annual Booklyn Zine Fundraiser Collection.

Work to highlight

While Booklyn isn't a zine library, they are an active organization in the zine scene in New York City, which is why they are included in this meta library.

Booklyn has an online catalog where zines are sold. The prices may be a bit out of range for someone who isn't a collector of zines, however, it is nice that one can still view the content for inspiration.

Booklyn offers various programming to youth and adults. Additionally, they have free resources including a podcast, a manual, and various tutorials.
